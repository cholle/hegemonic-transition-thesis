# David P. Calleo — *Beyond American Hegemony: The Future of the Western Alliance* (1987)

**Source:** David P. Calleo, *Beyond American Hegemony: The Future of the Western Alliance* (New York: Basic Books / Twentieth Century Fund, 1987)

**Also referenced:** Calleo, *The Imperious Economy* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1982) — the prior volume establishing the economic analysis in detail.

---

## Central Thesis

The postwar American-led international order is being destroyed not by external challengers but by the hegemon's own refusal to adjust to a more plural distribution of global power. The United States, rather than accommodating rising powers and devolving responsibilities, has used its lingering institutional dominance — particularly the dollar's reserve-currency role and the NATO protectorate — to exploit the international system, compensating for its own declining relative strength. This exploitation, not Soviet aggression or allied weakness, is the primary source of systemic instability.

Calleo's master formula: **a hegemon in decay converts its institutional position from a vehicle for managing the system in the general interest into a vehicle for extracting resources to compensate for its own weakening.** The system then breaks down not because no one is in charge but because the one in charge is using the system as a personal life-support machine.

---

## The Hegemon in Decay: Calleo's Core Concept

This is Calleo's load-bearing theoretical contribution. He inverts the standard hegemonic stability argument (Kindleberger's claim that systems collapse from the absence of a stabilizer) by showing that **hegemonic systems are themselves inherently destabilizing** — specifically, that the mechanism of breakdown is the hegemon's own behavior as it declines.

The logic runs in stages:

1. **Hegemonic systems carry the seeds of their own erosion.** A liberal hegemon's success in promoting prosperity and development among its allies inevitably strengthens them relative to the leader. American policy fostered European and Japanese recovery; that recovery necessarily diminished American preponderance. The decline is not absolute failure — it is the predictable consequence of success.

2. **The hegemon faces a choice: adjust or exploit.** It can accept the trend toward pluralism and devolve responsibility. Or it can use its still-dominant institutional position (reserve currency, alliance structures, military basing) to extract resources and maintain its role beyond its real capacity. The second option is always politically easier — it defers costs and avoids the domestic disruption of structural adjustment.

3. **Exploitation accelerates breakdown.** The system's remaining members bear the costs of the hegemon's institutional parasitism. They eventually resist, defect, or build alternatives. The hegemon's response to their resistance is typically to tighten its grip, which accelerates the defection cycle.

4. **The danger is not the collapse of hegemony but the attempt to preserve it.** Calleo's most counterintuitive claim: the real risk to systemic stability is not that American hegemony will end but that the US will refuse to let it end gracefully.

## The Three Monetary Formulas: Mechanisms of Extraction

Calleo's most detailed empirical contribution is his analysis of three successive formulas by which the US has exploited the international monetary system to externalize its domestic imbalances. Each formula is a specific mechanism by which the hegemon converts institutional position into resource extraction:

### 1. The Bretton Woods Formula (1958–1971)

The dollar's role as the fixed-point reserve currency allowed the US to run chronic balance-of-payments deficits — effectively borrowing from the world by issuing dollars that other countries were obligated to hold. European central banks accumulated dollar balances that amounted to forced loans, often financing activities Europeans opposed (Vietnam, US corporate acquisitions in Europe). French critics (especially Jacques Rueff, channeled through de Gaulle) identified the gold-exchange standard as inherently inflationary and exploitative: it uncoupled US monetary policy from the adjustment mechanisms that disciplined every other country.

The system broke down when accumulating dollar liabilities exceeded US gold reserves, speculation against the dollar became rational, and Nixon was forced to float (1971) and then formally devalue (1973).

### 2. The Nixon Formula (1971–1979)

Floating rates freed the US from the obligation to defend the dollar's fixed value, allowing continued deficit spending financed by deliberate depreciation. The falling dollar effectively devalued America's debts to foreign holders while making US exports more competitive. The "locomotive theory" — strong economies should reflate to pull the world out of recession — became the ideological cover for what was in practice a policy of exporting inflation. Europeans, particularly the Germans, bore the costs through imported inflation, undermined export competitiveness, and the impossibility of independent monetary policy so long as the dollar remained the system's dominant currency.

The formula collapsed in 1979 when European, OPEC, and domestic capital-market revolts converged: the dollar crashed, gold spiked, bond markets nearly collapsed, and OPEC imposed its second real price increase.

### 3. The Reagan Formula (1980–1987+)

The most sophisticated and destructive variant. Volcker's tight money combined with Reagan's massive fiscal deficits (driven primarily by military spending) to produce very high real interest rates. These attracted a massive net capital inflow to the US, financing the twin deficits (budget and trade) while pushing the dollar to record heights. The US was simultaneously borrowing from the world, devastating its own manufacturing competitiveness, and extracting capital from Europe and the developing world.

Calleo's key insight: each formula eventually reaches its inherent limits and is replaced by the next, but the underlying structural problem — the attempt to maintain hegemonic commitments beyond America's real fiscal capacity — remains constant. The formulas are not solutions; they are successive methods of deferral. And each deferral makes the eventual reckoning worse.

The sequence of formulas demonstrates a ratchet effect: **Bretton Woods** = borrowing via institutional privilege → **Nixon** = borrowing via currency depreciation → **Reagan** = borrowing via capital-market manipulation. Each requires more aggressive exploitation of the system and produces more damaging side effects.

## The Fiscal-Military Nexus

Calleo's structural diagnosis connects the monetary exploitation directly to military overextension. The causal chain:

1. NATO and global military commitments (of which NATO was ~58% of the defense budget by Pentagon estimates) impose costs that exceed what the American political system is willing to tax for.
2. The resulting structural fiscal deficit is the root cause of the chronic balance-of-payments deficit.
3. The balance-of-payments deficit is managed through successive monetary formulas that exploit the dollar's international role.
4. The monetary exploitation destabilizes the international economy, which in turn weakens the alliance that the military commitments were supposed to protect.

The vicious circle: **hegemony requires military spending that produces fiscal deficits that require monetary exploitation that undermines the system that hegemony is supposed to stabilize.** The American political system cannot resolve this because it simultaneously demands the global role and refuses to pay for it. Neither raising taxes to hegemonic-commitment levels nor reducing commitments to taxable levels has ever been politically achievable.

Calleo's comparison with France and Germany is devastating: both maintained comparable per-capita GNPs, more generous welfare states, *and* balanced budgets — because their defense spending was a fraction of America's, subsidized by the very US protectorate whose costs were driving America into fiscal ruin.

## Devolution: The Prescription That Was Never Taken

Calleo's policy argument — that the US should devolve European defense responsibility to the Europeans — is the book's normative center. The argument is straightforward: Western Europe has the population, wealth, industrial capacity, and military traditions to manage its own defense. The notion that France and Germany together cannot balance Russia in Europe "strains credulity." The only reason Europeans haven't organized their own defense is that the American protectorate makes it unnecessary — and that protectorate persists not because Europe needs it but because American elites cannot imagine an alternative.

Devolution would permit: reduced US military budgets → fiscal balance → monetary stability → stable exchange rates → rational investment patterns → sustainable world economic growth. Calleo is not arguing for American withdrawal from Europe, but for transformation of the alliance from a protectorate into a genuine partnership.

**Why it didn't happen** is Calleo's most bitter observation. The American political system is not blocked by public opposition to devolution — the public has never been presented with the option. It is blocked by elite consensus. American foreign-policy elites remain "enthralled by hegemony and duopoly." They can imagine reaffirmation or retreat, but not a structured sharing of responsibility. The result is that "American policy, requiring a supremacy that cannot be sustained, lacks the guiding principles needed to determine priorities. Despite its colossal military means, America feels perpetually threatened and overextended."

## The Pluralist Counter-Thesis

Calleo's deepest theoretical move is to invert the relationship between hegemonic and pluralist systems. Standard hegemonic stability theory (Kindleberger, Gilpin) holds that plural systems are inherently unstable and that hegemonic leadership is necessary for order. Calleo argues the opposite: **hegemonic systems are inherently unstable because the hegemon's inevitable decline leads to exploitative behavior that destroys the system from within.** Plural systems, where multiple powers share responsibility and internalize the system's constraints, are potentially more stable — if they can be achieved.

His historical case: the interwar breakdown was caused not by the absence of hegemony but by the destructive behavior of *two* hegemonic powers — Britain in decay and America on the rise. Both manipulated the gold-exchange standard for national advantage. Both refused to play by the rules their system required. The gold-exchange standard collapsed not from lack of a leader but from the leaders' exploitation of their institutional positions. "It is not pluralistic systems that are unstable, but hegemonic systems."

This places Calleo in a distinctive theoretical position: he shares Kindleberger's diagnosis of the 1929-33 breakdown (monetary system failure, no stabilizer) but attributes the *cause* differently. For Kindleberger, the problem is the absence of leadership. For Calleo, the problem is the presence of a decaying leader who is using the system to compensate for its own weakness.

## Relationship to the Thesis Framework

### Where Calleo Fits in the Framework Table

| Framework | Contribution | Key Concept |
|---|---|---|
| **Calleo** (hegemonic decline) | The political-economic mechanism of incumbent breakdown: declining powers convert institutional position from system management to resource extraction; this resistance to adjustment is the primary driver of systemic instability | Hegemon in decay; exploitative hegemony; devolution; three monetary formulas |

### The Calleo–Hudson Synthesis

Calleo and Hudson are analyzing the same phenomenon from complementary angles. Hudson's "super imperialism" describes the *institutional architecture* of extraction (Treasury-bill standard, IMF conditionality, military basing as protection racket). Calleo provides the *political-economic dynamic* — the explanation of *why* the hegemon turns predatory. For Hudson, the US designed the postwar system to extract from the beginning. For Calleo, extraction is a *degeneration* — the system was originally designed to manage the general interest, but the hegemon's relative decline converted management into exploitation.

The thesis benefits from holding both: Hudson is right that the institutional architecture was always loaded in America's favor (the Bretton Woods system was built on American terms). But Calleo adds the temporal dimension — the *degree* and *character* of exploitation intensified as relative decline accelerated. The three formulas trace the escalation. What was tolerable institutional privilege in the 1950s (when the US was genuinely providing systemic public goods) became exploitative by the 1960s, predatory by the 1970s, and structurally destructive by the 1980s.

The current moment (2025-26) represents what might be called a **fourth formula**: the Trump-era weaponization of the dollar system itself (sanctions, SWIFT exclusion, asset freezes) combined with direct military asset-stripping (Venezuela's oil, attempted regime change in Iran). This is no longer even the sophisticated financial exploitation Calleo described — it is the naked conversion of institutional position into coercive extraction, Hudson's "protection racket" without the residual pretense of systemic management.

### The Calleo–Arrighi Connection

Arrighi's systemic cycles of accumulation provide the macro-pattern; Calleo supplies the micro-mechanism of the transition phase. Arrighi identifies the shift from material expansion to financial expansion as the "sign of autumn" for each hegemonic cycle. Calleo explains *how* the financial turn works politically: the hegemon, unable to compete productively, uses its residual institutional dominance (particularly monetary) to extract resources through financial manipulation. Each of Calleo's three formulas is a specific variant of Arrighi's financial-expansion phase.

The connection is precise: Arrighi's "domination without hegemony" (borrowed from Gramsci via Guha) is exactly Calleo's "hegemon in decay converting management into exploitation." Arrighi cites Calleo directly, and Calleo's empirical work on the monetary formulas is one of the primary evidential bases for Arrighi's theoretical claim about the US financial turn.

Key difference: Arrighi sees the pattern as a recurring systemic cycle with the possibility of a new hegemon (China) resolving the chaos phase. Calleo's analysis, focused on the Atlantic relationship, doesn't address the succession question — he prescribes pluralist devolution rather than hegemonic replacement. This makes Calleo structurally closer to Acharya's multiplex endpoint than to Arrighi's cyclical succession.

### The Calleo–Kindleberger Inversion

This is the framework's most productive tension at the theoretical level. Kindleberger and Calleo share the same empirical object (the interwar monetary collapse and its postwar parallels) but reach opposite conclusions about causation:

- **Kindleberger:** The system collapsed because no one was willing to stabilize it. Britain couldn't; America wouldn't. The lesson: systems need a hegemon.
- **Calleo:** The system collapsed because the hegemonic powers *destabilized* it through their own exploitative behavior. Britain and America corrupted the gold-exchange standard. The lesson: hegemonic systems self-destruct.

For the thesis, this tension is not a contradiction to resolve but a diagnostic tool. Both can be true simultaneously and at different scales: Kindleberger identifies the immediate precipitating cause (no one providing systemic public goods during the crisis), while Calleo identifies the structural cause (the hegemon's pre-crisis exploitation created the conditions that made the crisis inevitable and unmanageable). Applied to 2025-26: Kindleberger explains why the Iran/Gulf crisis cascades (no stabilizer providing counter-cyclical lending or open markets for distress goods). Calleo explains why the US is not *acting* as stabilizer even though it retains the capacity (it has converted the stabilizer role into an extraction role, and providing public goods would require sacrificing the extraction).

### Calleo and Perez: The Fiscal-Technological Connection

Calleo's fiscal-military nexus connects to Perez through the resource-allocation question. Perez's Turning Point — the institutional reform that channels the new techno-economic paradigm from speculative Installation to productive Deployment — requires the hegemon to redirect resources from financial speculation to productive investment. Calleo's analysis shows precisely why the US hegemon *cannot* make this pivot: its fiscal resources are locked into military overextension, its monetary system is structured to extract rather than invest, and its political system is incapable of either reducing commitments or raising taxes.

The 5th surge's failed Turning Point (Perez's diagnosis) is, in Calleo's terms, a direct consequence of the fiscal-military-monetary vicious circle. The US cannot fund a New Deal–style deployment because its budget is consumed by the military infrastructure of a hegemonic role it can no longer afford. The current attempts to redirect rent extraction from energy to technology (chips, AI, IP) are not the institutional reform Perez's Turning Point requires — they are a new application of Calleo's exploitation formula to a new domain.

### Calleo and Polanyi: Exploitative Hegemony as Disembedding

Calleo's description of the hegemon exploiting the international system maps onto Polanyi's concept of disembedding. The three monetary formulas are successive episodes of the international economy being disembedded from the social and political institutions that were supposed to regulate it. Each formula represents the US using its institutional position to override the adjustment mechanisms (gold flows, exchange-rate corrections, fiscal discipline) that constrain every other economy. The social costs of this disembedding are borne by the system's other members — European unemployment, Third World debt crises, developing-country austerity — generating the "double movement" counter-responses that Polanyi predicts: European monetary integration (the EMS, eventually the euro), OPEC price solidarity, import substitution industrialization, and ultimately the rent-avoidance alternatives the thesis tracks.

### Calleo and Olson: The Institutional Sclerosis Problem

Calleo's observation that American elites "remain enthralled by hegemony" despite its evident self-destructiveness maps directly onto Olson's distributional coalitions. The foreign-policy establishment, the defense industry, Wall Street's international operations, and the military-basing complex constitute precisely the kind of entrenched distributional coalition Olson describes: they capture the policy process, resist adjustment, and impose costs on the broader society to preserve their particularistic advantages. Calleo notes that "nothing can correct American fiscal policy short of a general financial catastrophe" — which is Olson's prediction that institutional sclerosis persists until an exogenous shock forces restructuring.

### Calleo and Gramsci: The Legitimation Crisis

Calleo's distinction between hegemony-as-management and hegemony-as-exploitation maps precisely onto Gramsci's hegemony/domination distinction. The Pax Americana's early decades involved genuine leadership of a general interest (European recovery, open trade, monetary stability) — Gramscian consent-based hegemony. The shift to exploitative formulas represents the transition to domination: coercion and institutional leverage replacing consent and shared benefit. Calleo's observation that American allies "complain but manage" captures the transitional phase — the consent is eroding but hasn't yet collapsed. The current moment (2025-26), with Gulf states hit by Iranian missiles while hosting US bases, represents the full collapse of the consent structure that Calleo saw beginning to fracture.

## The Calleo Thesis Applied to the Rent-Avoidance Framework

Calleo provides the crucial mechanism that connects the thesis's original theoretical contributions:

**Why rent-avoidance occurs:** The hegemon in decay doesn't simply fail to provide public goods (Kindleberger). It actively converts its institutional position into an extraction apparatus (Calleo). This coercion — not mere neglect — is what compels subordinate actors to build alternatives. You don't build CIPS because SWIFT stopped working; you build it because SWIFT became a weapon. You don't develop EVs because ICE engines stopped functioning; you develop them because the ICE patent architecture became a rent-extraction chokepoint. Calleo's contribution is identifying the *active agency* in the process: it is the hegemon's resistance to adjustment that produces the conditions for its own displacement.

**Why creative obstruction occurs:** Calleo's analysis of the American political system's inability to devolve explains why the US doesn't simply adjust to the new paradigm. The distributional coalitions that benefit from the old arrangement (fossil fuels, defense contractors, financial sector, IP holders) have captured the policy process. They use state power to block the competitive dynamics that would destroy their position. This is creative obstruction: the active use of hegemonic institutions to prevent the new productive combinations from displacing incumbent rent streams. Calleo shows this is not merely greed or stupidity — it is structurally determined by the fiscal-military nexus. The old paradigm's rent streams *fund* the military commitments that sustain the institutional position that enables the rent extraction. Cutting any link in the chain threatens the whole structure.

**Why the process is self-defeating:** Calleo's most powerful contribution to the thesis is the demonstration that the hegemon's defense strategy accelerates its own displacement. Each successive monetary formula is more aggressive, more destabilizing, and more transparently exploitative than the last — and each provokes more determined resistance and alternative-building by the system's other members. The fourth formula (direct military asset-stripping) will produce more radical defection than the third (capital-market manipulation), just as the third produced more than the second (depreciation). The escalation is structurally determined: as softer forms of exploitation are exhausted, harder forms become necessary, but harder forms generate harder resistance. This is the Calleo thesis applied to paradigm transition: **coercive occupation of rent points accelerates the rent-avoidance behavior that undermines those positions.**

## Open Questions and Limitations

### 1. The Devolution Road Not Taken

Calleo's 1987 prescription — that the US should devolve European defense responsibility — was never implemented. Instead, NATO expanded eastward, the US assumed *more* commitments, and the fiscal-military vicious circle intensified. Did the end of the Cold War represent a missed opportunity for the pluralist adjustment Calleo prescribed? Or was the expansion itself another iteration of the exploitation formula — using the disappearance of the Soviet threat not to devolve but to lock in hegemonic position at lower cost? The thesis might productively frame NATO expansion as Calleo's worst-case scenario realized: the hegemon, rather than adjusting to a more plural world, used a geopolitical windfall to extend and entrench its institutional position.

### 2. The Missing Technology Dimension

Written in 1987, Calleo's analysis lacks any engagement with techno-economic paradigm change. His fiscal-military nexus is entirely within the 4th/5th Kondratiev wave framework. He does not anticipate how the energy transition, digitalization, or AI would reshape the terms of hegemonic competition. The thesis fills this gap by connecting Calleo's political-economic mechanism to Perez's technological revolution framework. But the connection needs to be made explicit: the fiscal resources locked into the military-hegemonic complex are not merely unavailable for Perez's deployment-phase institutional reform — they are actively directed toward preventing the new paradigm from displacing the old one's rent streams.

### 3. The Absence of the Global South

Calleo's analysis is almost entirely focused on the Atlantic relationship (US, Western Europe, with the Soviet Union as structural counterweight). The Third World appears primarily as a site of US-Soviet proxy competition and as a source of debt crises caused by the monetary formulas. There is no sustained engagement with how the Global South experiences and responds to exploitative hegemony on its own terms. The thesis's case studies (Venezuela, Iran) and the rent-avoidance framework fill this gap — but it's worth noting that Calleo's analytical architecture, while powerful, was built from a distinctly North Atlantic vantage point.

### 4. Calleo vs. Calleo: Pluralism or Hegemonic Succession?

There is an unresolved tension in Calleo's own framework. His pluralist prescription assumes that a genuinely balanced system of shared responsibility is achievable. But his analysis of how hegemonic systems degenerate applies just as well to any new arrangement: if a European defense community were established, wouldn't the strongest European power (Germany? France?) eventually begin exploiting its institutional position within the coalition, reproducing the same dynamic at a smaller scale? Calleo gestures at this with his discussion of the Franco-German relationship but never confronts it theoretically. The thesis, via Acharya's multiplex concept, takes this further: perhaps the endpoint is not a stable pluralism but an indefinite condition of overlapping, competing institutional arrangements — none dominant enough to exploit, none coherent enough to stabilize.

## What to Read

**Essential:**
- Calleo, *Beyond American Hegemony* (1987) — the core text. Chapters 6 ("The Atlantic Alliance and the World Economy"), 7 ("America's Budgetary Dilemma"), and 8 ("The Pax Americana and the Pax Britannica") carry the analytical weight. Now in project files (full PDF).
- Calleo, *The Imperious Economy* (1982) — the more detailed economic analysis. Not in project files but referenced throughout *Beyond American Hegemony*. Contains the extended treatment of the monetary formulas and the fiscal-military nexus.

**Pair with:**
- **Hudson**, *Super Imperialism* — the institutional-architecture complement. Where Calleo describes the political-economic *dynamic* of exploitation, Hudson maps the *institutional mechanisms*. Together they provide the thesis's complete account of how hegemonic extraction works.
- **Kindleberger**, *The World in Depression* — the theoretical counterpoint. Reading Kindleberger's hegemonic stability argument against Calleo's pluralist inversion sharpens both.
- **Arrighi**, *Adam Smith in Beijing* — the systemic-cycle framework that absorbs Calleo's analysis into a longer historical pattern.
