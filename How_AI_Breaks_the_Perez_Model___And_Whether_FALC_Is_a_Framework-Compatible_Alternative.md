# How AI Breaks the Perez Model — And Whether FALC Is a Framework-Compatible Alternative

## Framework Extension Document — March 2026

## PART I: HOW AI BREAKS THE PEREZ MODEL

### The Model's Logic and Its Hidden Assumption

Perez's four-phase surge model (Irruption → Frenzy → Turning Point → Synergy → Maturity) depends on a structural mechanism that has operated identically across all five surges since 1771: **the new techno-economic paradigm, once properly deployed, expands the wage base**.

This is not incidental to the model. It is the mechanism by which the Turning Point produces a golden age. The sequence works as follows:

1. Financial capital funds over-installation of new infrastructure during Frenzy
2. The crash at the Turning Point humbles financial capital and creates political space for institutional reform
3. Institutional recomposition redirects the economy toward **Deployment** — generalizing the paradigm across all sectors
4. Deployment expands demand by pulling successive layers of the population into productive employment within the new paradigm
5. This demand expansion absorbs the enormous productive capacity installed during Frenzy, producing the golden age of widespread prosperity

Every prior paradigm followed this pattern because every prior paradigm **augmented human labor without replacing its primary input**:

- **Industrial Revolution**: Mechanized muscle power in textiles and iron — created factory employment on a massive scale
- **Steam & Railways**: Extended the reach of production and distribution — created construction, operation, and maintenance employment across entire national territories
- **Steel & Electricity**: Scaled production to continental scope — created heavy industry employment, electrical infrastructure jobs, engineering professions
- **Oil & Mass Production**: Fordism — the paradigm that most explicitly built the golden age on wage expansion. Mass production *requires* mass consumption; Henry Ford's insight was that workers must be paid enough to buy what they produce. The entire postwar welfare state was the institutional framework for this demand loop.
- **Information & Telecom**: This one is already anomalous — the 5th surge never achieved a proper Turning Point or golden age, producing instead extended financialization. But even ICT, in principle, created new job categories (software, networking, digital services) even as it destroyed old ones.

### The Structural Break

AI is qualitatively different. It does not augment a specific physical input (muscle, speed, reach, scale, information processing). **It replaces the primary input of all prior paradigms: human cognitive labor.**

Every previous paradigm's golden age was built on a specific version of the same structural fact: the new technology made human labor *more productive*, which meant more output per worker, which — with proper institutional arrangements — meant higher wages, which meant expanded demand, which meant the golden age feedback loop. The technology needed human workers and made them more valuable.

AI inverts this. For the first time, the paradigm-defining technology does not make human cognitive labor more productive in a way that requires more of it. It makes human cognitive labor *substitutable*. The technology becomes more capable precisely as it needs fewer humans.

This breaks the Perez model at its structural core — the Deployment mechanism:

| Perez Phase | Prior Paradigms | AI Paradigm |
|---|---|---|
| **Irruption** | New tech creates explosive new sectors, new entrepreneurs | Same — AI startups, venture capital frenzy |
| **Frenzy** | Financial capital decouples, casino economy, over-investment | Same — AI bubble dynamics already visible |
| **Turning Point** | Crash → institutional reform → demand expansion | Crash plausible — but **what institutional reform expands demand when the technology eliminates the jobs that generate wages?** |
| **Synergy/Golden Age** | Paradigm generalizes, employment expands, income distribution improves, widespread prosperity | **Cannot obtain** without an external mechanism that decouples income from employment |
| **Maturity** | Diminishing returns, market saturation, idle money | May arrive faster — AI-generated productivity gains with compressed wage base = faster market saturation |

### Why This Isn't Just Another "Automation Panic"

The standard response to AI displacement fears is the lump-of-labor fallacy: new technologies destroy some jobs but create others, and the net effect has always been positive. This is historically true for every Perez surge. But the argument rests on an assumption that AI specifically violates: **prior technologies created new categories of work because they augmented a dimension of human capacity while leaving other dimensions for humans to fill**.

Steam replaced muscle but created demand for human judgment in operating, maintaining, and managing the new systems. Mass production replaced craft skill but created demand for human coordination, design, marketing, and administration. ICT replaced calculation but created demand for human programming, analysis, communication, and creative work.

AI targets the residual category — the cognitive, communicative, analytical, creative capacities that previous paradigms left for humans to fill. There is no obvious "next layer up" that AI augments while leaving a new human niche. When the technology is general-purpose cognitive capability, the displacement logic is recursive: every new task created by AI deployment is itself a candidate for AI automation.

This does not mean zero employment. It means the wage base compresses even as productivity expands — which is precisely the structural condition Perez identifies as requiring institutional remedy at the Turning Point, except that now the institutional remedy cannot be "expand employment within the new paradigm" because **the paradigm's defining feature is that it doesn't need the employment**.

### What the Framework Convergence Says About This

The other frameworks in the project illuminate the AI-breaks-Perez problem from different angles:

**Hudson**: AI deployment under current institutional arrangements concentrates productivity gains as rent. Silicon Valley's business model is already Hudson's protection racket applied to cognitive labor — platform monopolies extract rents from the automation of tasks previously performed by wage workers. The super-imperialism thesis predicts that AI rents, like petrodollar rents, will be recycled into financial assets rather than productive investment, deepening the very financialization that prevented the 5th surge's golden age.

**Arrighi**: AI-driven productivity without wage expansion is the terminal form of the financial expansion phase. Capital can no longer profitably invest in production that employs people, so it invests in technology that replaces them — this is accumulation by dispossession applied to the cognitive labor force. Arrighi's Smithian vs. Schumpeterian distinction becomes critical: China's AI development path, embedded in a state that still prioritizes employment stability, may follow a different deployment logic than the US's, where financial capital's dominance ensures AI serves rent extraction rather than demand expansion.

**Wallerstein**: AI may be the mechanism through which the capitalist world-system's terminal crisis expresses itself technologically. Wallerstein's argument that capitalism itself (not just US hegemony) is in structural crisis gains specificity: the system cannot generate the institutional conditions for a new golden age because the technology that defines the new paradigm makes the golden age's structural prerequisite (wage-based demand expansion) impossible within capitalist relations of production. This is Marx's fetters thesis made concrete.

**Polanyi**: AI displacement is a new fictitious commodity crisis. Labor was always a fictitious commodity — treating human activity as if it were produced for sale. AI makes the fiction unsustainable by demonstrating that the "commodity" (human cognitive labor) is no longer scarce. Polanyi's double movement predicts a counter-movement — but its political form is radically uncertain. It could be UBI, it could be neo-feudal platform dependency, it could be authoritarian make-work, it could be genuine post-capitalist reorganization. The framework says a counter-movement *will* come but cannot specify its form.

**Gramsci**: The consent structure of liberal capitalism — "work hard, get ahead, consume" — collapses when the "work hard" part becomes structurally unavailable. What replaces it as the basis of social consent? This is the legitimation crisis that AI provokes, and it has no precedent in the Perez model because all prior paradigms could legitimate themselves through employment expansion.

**Marx (the thesis document's own framework)**: Relations of production becoming fetters on forces of production — AI is the most literal expression of this in history. The productive forces have developed to the point where they can operate without the labor input that defines capitalist value relations. The system produces its own gravediggers not as a class of workers but as a technology that makes the wage relation structurally unnecessary.

### The Diagnosis

AI breaks the Perez model because the model's Deployment phase requires institutional reforms that channel the new paradigm's productivity into demand expansion through employment. If the paradigm's defining capability is the replacement of human cognitive labor, no institutional reform *within* the existing framework can produce the demand expansion the model requires. The golden age mechanism is not delayed (as with the 5th surge's failed Turning Point) — it is structurally foreclosed unless a mechanism external to wage labor generates mass purchasing power.

This is the precise point where the frameworks converge on a problem none of them individually addresses: **the system needs a new basis for income distribution that is not wages, and no existing institutional architecture provides one**.

## PART II: FULLY AUTOMATED LUXURY COMMUNISM AND THE FRAMEWORK

### What FALC Actually Claims

Aaron Bastani's *Fully Automated Luxury Communism* (Verso, 2019) argues that technological development — in AI, renewable energy, synthetic biology, and space mining — is producing "extreme supply" that will eliminate scarcity in energy, labor, information, and eventually material resources. Under capitalism, this abundance is artificially restricted to maintain profit through scarcity. FALC proposes that political reorganization (worker-owned enterprises, universal basic services, public banking, "people's taxes" on resource extraction) can redirect technological abundance toward universal prosperity, making luxury available to all.

FALC draws explicitly on the Fragment on Machines from Marx's *Grundrisse* (Notebooks VI–VII, written 1857–58), the most heterodox passage in Marx's corpus. The key claims of the Fragment:

1. **The general intellect migrates into fixed capital.** As production becomes more scientific, knowledge itself — not labor — becomes the primary productive force. This knowledge is objectified in machinery ("organs of the human brain, created by the human hand; the power of knowledge, objectified").

2. **Direct labor becomes a minor input.** The worker is reduced to a "conscious linkage" in an automatic system, a watchman and regulator rather than the primary source of value creation.

3. **This undermines the value basis of capitalism.** Since capitalism measures wealth by labor time, and labor time is becoming irrelevant to actual production, "production based on exchange value breaks down." Capital becomes "the moving contradiction" — it drives to minimize labor time while positing labor time as the sole measure of wealth.

4. **The utopian resolution.** With automation, "disposable time" replaces labor time as the measure of social wealth. Work becomes more supervisory, more like play — observing automated processes rather than performing alienated labor. The "general reduction of the necessary labour of society to a minimum" corresponds to "the artistic, scientific etc. development of the individuals in the time set free."

FALC takes this vision and updates it: AI is the general intellect made literal. Solar energy is abundance of the primary physical input. Synthetic biology is abundance of food and medicine. The post-scarcity society Marx glimpsed in 1858 is now technologically achievable. What remains is the politics.

### How FALC Maps to Our Framework

The question is whether FALC fits, mitigates, or contradicts the analytical framework — and whether it occupies a position analogous to Öcalan's democratic confederalism, as a **planned alternative that emerges from the framework's diagnostic logic but operates at a normative/prescriptive register**.

#### Where FALC Fits the Framework

**FALC correctly identifies the structural problem.** The convergence of AI, energy transition, and biotech does represent what Perez would call the gestation of a potential 6th techno-economic paradigm — one whose productive potential radically exceeds the institutional capacity of existing capitalism to deploy it. The frameworks agree: the system cannot deploy the new paradigm's full potential through existing institutional arrangements.

**FALC correctly reads the Fragment on Machines as relevant.** Marx's argument that the general intellect migrates into fixed capital is the best available description of what AI actually does — it objectifies collective human cognitive capability in machinery. The Fragment's prediction that "direct labour and its quantity disappear as the determinant principle of production" is more plausible in 2026 than at any point since 1858.

**FALC correctly identifies the distributional crisis.** The thesis's own convergence diagnosis — that the turning point cannot be resolved through wage expansion when the paradigm eliminates wage labor — is FALC's starting point. Bastani's "extreme supply" is the demand-side consequence of the same problem Perez's model cannot resolve.

**FALC's reading of capitalist rent-extraction as artificial scarcity aligns with the framework.** The "creative obstruction" thesis — that American capitalism blocks new productive combinations to protect incumbent rent streams — is precisely FALC's argument that capitalism responds to abundance by imposing artificial scarcity through IP law, platform monopoly, and financial gatekeeping. Hudson's analysis of rent extraction maps directly onto Bastani's account of how technological abundance is captured and restricted.

#### Where FALC Contradicts or Fails the Framework

**FALC has no theory of power, transition, or class struggle.** This is the most damaging critique and the one that every serious reviewer identifies. The framework's convergence on the current moment diagnoses it as a period of systemic chaos (Wallerstein), domination without hegemony (Arrighi/Gramsci), and institutional sclerosis (Olson) — a moment when the forces capable of imposing institutional reform are precisely the forces most weakened by decades of financialization and labor displacement. FALC names the destination but has no map of the route through the chaos phase. Bastani's proposed mechanism — a social-democratic government implementing universal basic services — is strikingly inadequate to the scale of the structural transformation his own analysis implies.

**FALC is technologically deterministic in a way the framework explicitly rejects.** Perez's entire model rests on the insight that technological capability does not automatically produce institutional adaptation — that the Turning Point is a political struggle, not a technical outcome. Wallerstein's terminal crisis thesis emphasizes that the system's trajectory is radically underdetermined at the bifurcation point. Polanyi's double movement shows that counter-movements take unpredictable political forms. FALC implicitly assumes that technological abundance creates the conditions for its own political realization, which is the error the framework is designed to diagnose and avoid.

**FALC misreads the Fragment on Machines in a way that matters.** Michael Heinrich's influential critique (which tracks the evolution from the *Grundrisse* to *Capital*) argues that Marx himself abandoned the Fragment's logic: the separation of intellectual potentialities from workers is a tendency *immanent to all capitalist production*, not a tipping point that puts capitalism into question. In *Capital*, automation does not undermine value production — it restructures it. This matters for the framework because it means the Marxian basis for FALC's optimism (automation dissolves the value relation) may be weaker than Bastani assumes. Capital has proven extraordinarily capable of imposing the value form on activities that don't "need" it — bullshit jobs, artificial scarcity, platform rent-seeking are all examples of the system maintaining the wage relation long after its productive necessity has diminished.

**FALC ignores the geopolitical dimension entirely.** The framework tracks a hegemonic transition in which the deployment of the new paradigm is inseparable from the question of who controls it. FALC operates as if technology is geopolitically neutral — as if solar abundance and AI capability are equally available everywhere and the only question is domestic political will. The framework's analysis of Venezuela, Iran, and the US military defense of the old energy paradigm shows how far this is from reality. The deployment of the post-scarcity paradigm is itself a site of great-power competition, not a neutral process waiting for the right politics.

**FALC shares an extractivist ontology with capitalism.** The most penetrating critique from ecological and decolonial reviewers: Bastani's vision of abundance (asteroid mining, synthetic biology, unlimited solar) treats nature as an infinitely exploitable resource — which is the same ontological assumption that produced the ecological crisis. Post-scarcity achieved through unbounded extraction is not post-capitalist in any deep sense; it is hypercapitalism with better distribution. The framework, especially through Polanyi's analysis of land as a fictitious commodity, provides resources to identify this as a structural limitation rather than a minor oversight.

### FALC's Position in the Framework: An Analogue to Öcalan?

The Acharya analysis document established a category for thinkers who **operate at a different register than the analytical framework — prescriptive where the thesis is diagnostic**. Öcalan and Bookchin were placed in this category: they do not belong in the main convergence table, but they serve the specific functions of (a) specifying what Acharya's multiplex world leaves unspecified and (b) providing a live case study (Rojava) of the framework's predictions at the micro-institutional level.

**FALC fits this category, but less rigorously than Öcalan/Bookchin, and with a different structural position.**

Here is the analogy:

| | **Öcalan/Bookchin** | **FALC** |
|---|---|---|
| **What it specifies** | What Acharya's multiplex looks like as institutional design — the political architecture of a post-hegemonic order | What the post-Perez deployment looks like when the golden age mechanism is foreclosed — the distributional architecture of a post-wage economy |
| **Diagnostic origin** | Wallerstein's terminal crisis + Acharya's multiplexity = what are the institutional forms? | Perez's broken deployment + Marx's Fragment = what are the distributional forms? |
| **Live case study** | Rojava — confederalism under military pressure in the thesis's active theater | None — FALC has no operating instance. This is its most serious deficiency compared to Öcalan. |
| **Framework relationship** | Prescriptive where framework is diagnostic; does not contradict framework analysis | Prescriptive where framework is diagnostic; **partially contradicts** framework on technological determinism and extractivist ontology |
| **Analytical rigor** | Bookchin developed libertarian municipalism as a general institutional theory; Öcalan adapted it to specific conditions | Bastani presents a journalistic manifesto; no rigorous institutional theory. Criticized for lacking a theory of power, class struggle, and transition. |
| **Intellectual lineage quality** | Bookchin → Öcalan → Rojava = theory → adaptation → implementation | Fragment on Machines → autonomist Marxists → FALC = unfinished notebook → Italian theory → pop manifesto. The intellectual chain has weakened at each step. |

### The Better Entry Point: Virno, Not Bastani

If the framework needs to incorporate the Fragment on Machines / post-scarcity logic, the stronger intellectual entry points are not FALC but the autonomist tradition that FALC popularizes:

- **Paolo Virno** ("Notes on the General Intellect," 1996) — makes the critical correction to Marx that FALC doesn't: the general intellect manifests not only as fixed capital (machinery) but as **living labor** — as the linguistic, communicative, cooperative capacities of workers themselves. This means AI doesn't simply objectify the general intellect in machines; it *also* targets the living-labor form of the general intellect that post-Fordist capitalism already exploits. This is analytically more precise than Bastani.

- **Nick Dyer-Witheford** (*Cyber-Marx*, 1999; *Cyber-Proletariat*, 2015) — situates the Fragment within a world-systems framework that is directly compatible with the thesis. Tracks how automation operates differently in core, semi-periphery, and periphery. Provides the geopolitical dimension FALC lacks.

- **Shoshana Zuboff** (*The Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, 2019) — already on the recommended sources list. Provides the rent-extraction analysis specific to AI/data that the framework lacks. Where Hudson explains how the dollar system extracts surplus, Zuboff explains how the data system extracts behavioral surplus — a directly analogous mechanism for the AI era.

### The Verdict

**FALC identifies a real structural problem that the framework has not yet addressed — the foreclosure of the Perezian golden age mechanism by AI's labor-displacement logic.** In this sense, it is a productive provocation.

**But FALC fails as framework-compatible theory for four reasons:**
1. No theory of power or transition (the framework's entire strength is diagnosing why structural conditions don't automatically produce structural outcomes — Olson, Kindleberger, Polanyi)
2. Technological determinism that contradicts the framework's core insight (technology ≠ institutional adaptation)
3. No geopolitical analysis (the framework's active analytical theater is absent from FALC)
4. Extractivist ontology incompatible with Polanyi's fictitious commodities analysis

**Its proper position is as a footnote/reference point, not a framework addition.** Unlike Öcalan/Bookchin, FALC does not provide a rigorous institutional design that specifies what the framework leaves unspecified. It provides a slogan and a mood — useful for orienting popular political imagination, but not load-bearing for analytical work.

**The Fragment on Machines, however, *does* belong in the framework** — not via FALC but via the direct Marxian lineage. The Fragment's insight that general social knowledge becomes a direct force of production, objectified in machinery, is the most precise theoretical description available of what AI actually represents. Its internal contradiction — capital driving to minimize labor time while positing labor time as the sole measure of wealth — is the structural contradiction that breaks the Perez model.

## OPEN QUESTIONS FOR FURTHER DEVELOPMENT

1. **Is China's AI deployment path Smithian or Schumpeterian?** If China embeds AI within state-directed employment maintenance (retraining, make-work, platform regulation), does it produce a quasi-golden-age that the US cannot, because financial capital's dominance in the US ensures AI serves rent extraction? This is the Arrighi question applied to AI.

2. **Does AI accelerate or retard de-dollarization?** If AI-driven productivity is concentrated in US firms (OpenAI, Google, Meta, Microsoft), does this create a new form of dollar hegemony — an "AI standard" analogous to the petrodollar standard — that temporarily reverses the decline the framework tracks? Or does open-source AI (DeepSeek, Llama) distribute the general intellect beyond US control in the same way that solar panel manufacturing distributed energy production beyond OPEC control?

3. **Is the Fragment on Machines actually Marx's position, or a discarded notebook hypothesis?** Heinrich's argument that Marx abandoned this logic in *Capital* matters: if the mature Marx concluded that automation restructures rather than dissolves the value relation, then the FALC/autonomist optimism is built on a foundation Marx himself rejected. The framework should note this ambiguity rather than resolve it prematurely.

4. **What is the Polanyian counter-movement to AI displacement?** The double movement predicts a protective response. Its form is undetermined — could be UBI, could be neo-feudal platform dependency (gig economy as Speenhamland 2.0), could be authoritarian nationalism (MAGA as Polanyian counter-movement against the AI-driven dissolution of the employment contract), could be genuine post-capitalist reorganization. The framework should track this as an open empirical question.
