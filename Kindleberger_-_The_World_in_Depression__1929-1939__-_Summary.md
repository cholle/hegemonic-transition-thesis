# Kindleberger — *The World in Depression, 1929-1939* (1973)

**Source:** Charles P. Kindleberger, *The World in Depression, 1929-1939*, Volume 4 of *History of the World Economy in the Twentieth Century* (University of California Press, 1973/1975)

---

## Central Thesis

The 1929 depression was so **wide, so deep, and so long** because the international economic system was rendered unstable by **British inability and United States unwillingness** to assume responsibility for stabilizing it in three particulars:

1. **Maintaining a relatively open market for distress goods**
2. **Providing counter-cyclical long-term lending**
3. **Discounting in crisis**

The shocks themselves — overproduction of primary products, the halt of lending to Germany in 1928, the stock market crash — were not extraordinary. Similar magnitude shocks had been absorbed before (1920, 1927). The system was unstable because no country was stabilizing it.

> "In 1929, the British couldn't and the United States wouldn't. When every country turned to protect its national private interest, the world public interest went down the drain, and with it the private interests of all."

This is the origin of **hegemonic stability theory** — the argument that the international economic system requires a single leader willing to bear disproportionate costs to provide systemic public goods.

---

## Rejecting Monocausal Explanations

Kindleberger systematically dismantles uni-causal accounts:

- **Friedman (monetarist):** US monetary policy errors caused it. Kindleberger: "national, monetary, related to a policy decision. It is uni-causal. In my judgement it is wrong."
- **Hoover:** Origin lay in European postwar maladjustment, accentuated by 1931 financial crisis.
- **European consensus:** Depression started in the US — unwillingness to write off war debts, stock-market crash of 1929, frenzied lending of 1927-28.
- **Samuelson:** "A series of historical accidents." Kindleberger finds this unsatisfying because great depressions recur with disconcerting regularity (1873, 1848, 1826).
- **Robbins:** Misuse of the gold standard.
- **Hansen:** Secular stagnation / exhaustion of investment opportunities.
- **Svennilson:** Structural disequilibrium.

The chapter is deliberately titled "An Explanation" not "The Explanation."

## The Asymmetry Problem

If the world economy behaved symmetrically, there could be no world depression. Gold losses for one country would be deflationary, but gains for the recipient would yield offsetting expansion. But symmetry is not how the system works.

**19th-century British stabilization:** Britain maintained countercyclical lending — domestic recession stimulated foreign lending; domestic boom cut lending but expanded imports, providing export stimulus abroad. This counterpoint stabilized the system from 1873 to 1913.

**1920s US destabilization:** US foreign lending was **positively correlated** with domestic investment, not counterpoised. The boom and foreign lending went together; when the depression came, both collapsed simultaneously. The US cut imports and lending at the same time — the fundamental destabilizing fact.

**The stock market diversion:** From spring 1928 onward, the stock market boom diverted funds from foreign bonds into domestic speculation. This produced a roughly **$2 billion swing** in US lending that hammered peripheral and debtor countries *before the crash even happened*. Call-money loans rose from $4.4B (end 1927) to $6.4B (end 1928). Railroads and industrial companies turned from real investment to call loans — safe, liquid (it was thought), and high-yielding.

**The stock market as credit absorber:** While a priori reasoning suggests stock markets leave money supply unchanged (every buyer has a seller), in practice the boom diverted funds from the "transactions circulation" (money used for production, distribution, consumption) to the "financial circulation" (funds held for speculation). In an open economy, this also drained funds internationally — foreigners bought US securities, and the halt to foreign lending cut off expected capital flows to debtor nations.

## The Leadership Vacuum

### British Decline

- League of Nations programs for Austrian and Hungarian stabilization were largely British in spirit
- Dawes and Young Plans for German reparations were dominated by British experts
- By 1931, British capacity for leadership was gone
- French sterling balances constrained Britain as lender of last resort
- The climax of weakness: Norman offered 50 million schillings ($7 million) as a one-week loan during the 1931 Austrian Kreditanstalt crisis
- After the World Economic Conference of 1933, Britain turned inward — cultivating the Commonwealth and managed sterling
- The overvalued pound (restored to prewar parity in 1925) was a structural drag throughout

### US Isolationism

- New York (Fed, financial community) had interest in European affairs, but the country as a whole refused leadership
- "World leadership was offered, by almost universal consent, to the United States... [and] was declined" (E.H. Carr)
- **Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930):** Paradigmatic act of irresponsibility — signed despite 30+ formal foreign protests and advice of 1,000 economists. Triggered stampede of retaliatory protectionism. Important "less for its impact on the United States balance of payments, or as conduct unbecoming a creditor nation, than for its irresponsibility."
- The US refused to serve as lender of last resort in 1931
- Not until 1942 (Harry D. White preparing for Bretton Woods) did the US begin building a world system

### France: Destabilizer Without Stabilizing Power

- Not big enough to have responsibility forced on it, not small enough for irresponsibility to be costless
- Had the power to destabilize but was insufficiently powerful to stabilize
- Intransigence on reparations; political conditions attached to Austrian and German loans in 1931
- Bank of France converted dollars to gold in 1931-32 while protesting cooperation
- Rivalry with Bank of England over leadership in Eastern European stabilization risked systemic instability

### Small Countries

- Lacked power to affect outcomes — privileged to look after national interest
- Alternatively, held to the Kantian Categorical Imperative: act only in ways that can be generalized
- Their conversions of sterling to gold in 1931 and rapid tariff increases contributed to the spiral

## Structural Inheritance (Initial Conditions)

The "initial conditions" from which the depression emerged:

- **Reparations and war debts:** No equitable solution possible. The system inevitably ran down to wipe the slate clean of reparations, debts, and service on commercial lending.
- **Exchange rate misalignment:** Overvalued pound, undervalued franc — resulting in massive French claims on Britain.
- **German inflation trauma (1923):** Made Germany paranoid in its attachment to deflation.
- **Structural overproduction:** Wheat, sugar, wool, ships, cotton textiles, coal — of less consequence and manageable by the price system if macroeconomic stability could have been preserved.
- **Wage rigidity:** After ~1921, labor increasingly unwilling to accept wage decreases — making for irreversibility of wage and price increases. The 1920-21 deflation was the last time wages could be compressed so far and so fast.
- **US entry into world lending:** Substituting in part for Britain, "with much enthusiasm, no experience, and little in the way of guiding principles."

---

## The Positive Feedback Mechanism

Once the system began running down, negative programs (tariff truces, halting competitive depreciation) could only slow decline without **reversing the positive feedback mechanism**. Each country's attempt to protect its national private interest destroyed the world public interest.

- The attempt of interlocking multilateral trade partners to all achieve export surpluses wiped out all trade as each cut imports from the next
- The "contracting spiral of world trade" from January 1929 to March 1933
- Exchange depreciation to raise domestic prices: gain for one country was loss for all
- With tariff retaliation and competitive depreciation, mutual losses were certain
- The fallacy of composition: the whole less than the sum of its parts

---

## The 1929 Crash as Process

Kindleberger argues the crash was not a superficial phenomenon but **part of the deflationary mechanism**:

- The crash led to a scramble for liquidity by both call-market lenders and stock owners
- Orders cancelled, loans called
- Fed open-market purchases and discount rate cuts restored credit markets quickly
- But by then deflation had been communicated to fragile commodity markets and durable-goods industries
- The crash is "less interesting for the irony it permits the historian, bemused by the foibles of greedy men, than for starting a process which took on a dynamic of its own"

The 1937 recession within the depression demonstrated that the recovery was fragile and dependent on continued fiscal/monetary support — providing the intellectual basis for Keynesian stabilization.

## Relevance to Current Analytical Frameworks

### Hudson (Dollar Hegemony / Protection Racket)

Kindleberger describes exactly what Hudson theorizes — the hegemonic stabilizer provides public goods (open markets, counter-cyclical lending, crisis discounting). When the hegemon stops providing these and turns to narrow national interest, the system collapses. The Smoot-Hawley tariff is the 1930s equivalent of weaponized economic policy. The distinction between leadership as "provision of the public good of responsibility" versus "exploitation of followers or the private good of prestige" maps directly to Hudson's framework of hegemony-as-protection-racket.

### Arrighi (Systemic Cycles of Accumulation)

Kindleberger's leadership transition from Britain to the US is precisely Arrighi's hegemonic transition, with the interwar period as the **chaos phase** between British terminal decline and US assumption of the role (not fully until Bretton Woods/Marshall Plan). The positive correlation of US lending with domestic investment rather than counter-cyclical patterns marks the **immaturity of the new hegemon** — it had not yet internalized the rules of systemic leadership. Britain's shift from production to finance (already evident in declining growth after 1873 but maintained free trade from cultural lag) prefigures Arrighi's financial expansion as sign of autumn.

### Wallerstein (World-Systems / Terminal Crisis)

Kindleberger notes the crisis of 1848 may parallel 1929 — "both represented failures of the economic system at a transitional stage from one set of institutions and forms to another." This resonates with Wallerstein's argument about capitalism's structural crises during systemic transitions.

### Gramsci (Hegemony vs. Domination)

Kindleberger explicitly distinguishes leadership-as-responsibility from leadership-as-domination. His observation that US leadership was "beginning to slip" (written 1973) and the question of whether anyone would assume the stabilizing role maps to Gramsci's concept of hegemonic crisis — the point where consent structures break down and domination replaces hegemony.

### Current Application (2025-26)

Kindleberger's three unstable outcomes are directly applicable:

1. **US and rival vying for leadership** → US-China competition
2. **One unable and the other unwilling** (as in 1929-33) → China gaining capacity but not yet willing/able to provide systemic public goods; US retreating from stabilizer role
3. **Mutual vetoes without positive programs** → BRICS vs G7 paralysis

The deepest connection: the chaos phase isn't just about who has power — it's about whether anyone is willing to bear the costs of providing **systemic public goods**. The Venezuela/Iran interventions raise the question: is the US providing stability or asset-stripping the old order? Iran's missile strikes on Gulf states hosting US bases = the consent structure collapsing in real time, exactly as Kindleberger would predict when the hegemon prioritizes national interest over systemic stability.

### Perez (Techno-Economic Paradigm Shifts)

Kindleberger's account of the 1920s boom in automobiles, radios, refrigerators, motion pictures, and installment credit describes the **deployment phase** of the 4th techno-economic surge — with the crash representing the failed turning point where financial capital was not redirected toward broad-based deployment. The current parallel: the 5th surge (ICT) never got its proper turning point either, and the 6th (energy/AI) is gestating amid similar financial dominance over production.

## Key Quotes for Reference

On the central thesis:
> "It is the theme of this book that part of the reason for the length, and most of the explanation for the depth of the world depression, was the inability of the British to continue their role of underwriter to the system and the reluctance of the United States to take it on until 1936."

On cooperation vs. leadership:
> "If the United States does not take the leadership, nothing happens. Leadership may lack followership, and foolish or even sensible proposals may be defeated through lack of support. But the most sensible proposals emanating from small countries are valueless if they lack the capacity to carry them out and fail to enlist the countries that do."

On the positive feedback loop:
> "In exactly the same way, the attempt of a system of countries with interlocking multilateral trade to achieve export surpluses tends to wipe out all trade as successive trading partners cut imports from the next country."

On France's position:
> "Not quite big enough to have responsibility forced on it, nor small enough to afford the luxury of irresponsibility, the French position in the inter-war period was unenviable. It had the power to act as a destabilizer, but was insufficiently powerful to stabilize."

On conventional wisdom:
> "It is clear that the leading theorists and practitioners of the 1930s were wrong on a number of issues, such as budget-balancing, and poor on therapy, but their diagnosis was not as weak as is widely believed."
