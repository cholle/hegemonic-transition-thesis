# Giovanni Arrighi — *Adam Smith in Beijing: Lineages of the Twenty-First Century* (Verso, 2007)

## Full Analytical Synthesis

---

## THE BOOK'S OVERALL THESIS

Arrighi is tracking two interconnected developments: the failure of the neoconservative Project for a New American Century, and China's emergence as leader of the East Asian economic renaissance. His central claim is that these two developments, taken together, make Adam Smith's 18th-century vision of an eventual equalization of power between Western and non-Western civilizations more plausible than at any point in the 250 years since *The Wealth of Nations*.

---

## PART I: ADAM SMITH AND THE NEW ASIAN AGE (Chapters 1–3)

The theoretical foundation. Arrighi recovers a Smith that is radically different from the neoliberal caricature. Smith was not a theorist of self-regulating markets or endless capitalist expansion — he was a theorist of the market as an *instrument of government*. Political economy for Smith was explicitly a branch of statecraft.

The critical distinction is between two developmental paths:

- **Smith's "natural" path** prioritizes agriculture, domestic trade, and the home market — with foreign trade emerging organically from surplus. This is the path China followed historically.
- **Smith's "unnatural" path** prioritizes foreign trade, manufacturing for export, and the carrying trade — the path Europe followed.

Crucially, Smith did *not* see the European path as superior. He saw both paths leading to a "stationary state" or high-level equilibrium, and actually preferred the natural path as better serving the national interest.

Arrighi then maps the distinction between **"Smithian" market-based development** (growth within a given social framework) and **"Schumpeterian/Marxian" capitalist development** (growth that destroys and reconstitutes the social framework through creative destruction). The capitalist path is distinguished not by having more capitalists, but by capitalists having greater power to impose their class interest over the national interest. As Braudel put it, capitalism triumphs when it *becomes* the state.

This sets up the central puzzle: the capitalist path generates a synergy between wealth and military power that gave Europe a decisive advantage. But does this advantage reproduce indefinitely, or does it eventually generate its own contradictions?

## PART II: TRACKING GLOBAL TURBULENCE (Chapters 4–6)

Arrighi recasts Robert Brenner's account of the post-1970s global economic downturn within his own framework of hegemonic cycles. Where Brenner sees persistent over-capacity and inter-capitalist competition as the root cause, Arrighi sees a broader *crisis of hegemony*.

### The Gramscian Distinction

The key analytical apparatus: Arrighi uses Gramsci's distinction between **hegemony** (domination + consent, where leadership is perceived as serving a general interest) and **mere domination** (rule by coercion alone). He distinguishes between:

- **"Signal crises"** — problems that are temporarily resolved
- **"Terminal crises"** — irreversible breakdowns of hegemonic order

### The US Hegemonic Cycle

- **Postwar boom (1950s–60s)**: Embedded in a "warfare-welfare state" on a global scale — military Keynesianism plus social Keynesianism, with US sponsorship of German and Japanese reindustrialization as the centerpiece.
- **Signal crisis (late 1960s–70s)**: Vietnam, dollar crisis, profitability crisis.
- **US response**: The monetarist counterrevolution of the 1980s, aggressive competition for global capital flows, escalation of the arms race with the USSR.
- **Belle époque (1990s)**: Temporary reflation of US wealth and power.

### Systemic Cycles of Accumulation

Arrighi identifies four cycles (Genoese-Iberian, Dutch, British, US), each following the pattern **M-C-M'**: material expansion (investment in trade and production) followed by financial expansion (when over-accumulated capital withdraws from production into finance). Financial expansion is "a sign of autumn" — it temporarily reflates the incumbent's power but deepens the underlying crisis. Every belle époque has been temporary because it shifts burdens onto subordinate groups rather than resolving the structural contradiction.

### Accumulation by Dispossession

Financial expansions entail *accumulation by dispossession* — surplus capital lent to governments and populations in financial straits, redistributing assets from borrowers to creditors. This is profitable but produces no resolution; it concentrates liquidity further while alienating dispossessed groups, provoking both profitability and legitimacy crises simultaneously.

### The Calleo Thesis

International systems break down not only because aggressive new powers challenge the existing order, but also because **declining powers, rather than adjusting and accommodating, try to cement their slipping hegemony into an exploitative domination**. In the current transition, there are no credible aggressive new military powers — the danger comes entirely from US resistance to adjustment.

## PART III: HEGEMONY UNRAVELING (Chapters 7–9)

### The Terminal Crisis

Arrighi argues that the Bush administration's response to 9/11 precipitated the *terminal crisis* of US hegemony. The Project for a New American Century was a high-risk gambit: if the US could control Iraq, move to Iran, dominate Caspian oil, it could control the "global oil spigot" and maintain leverage over European and East Asian competitors dependent on West Asian energy.

Harvey's formulation captures the logic: what better way to ward off economic competition than to control the key resource competitors depend on, using the one domain where the US remains supreme — military might?

### The Consent Problem

Military success was never sufficient to maintain hegemony in the Gramscian sense. You need consent, not just coercion. The invasion of Iraq was inscribed in a project of US global domination that made no pretense of serving a general interest. It created a "bond of resistance" between France, Germany, Russia, and China — the outlines of the Eurasian bloc Mackinder had warned could dominate the world geopolitically.

**Result**: US hegemony deflated into **"domination without hegemony."** The US remains the preeminent military power but has lost the capacity to lead — to make plausible the claim that its actions serve a general interest. It retains leverage through a "balance of terror" linking its economic policies to those of its foreign competitors and financiers (the mutual assured destruction of dollar holdings), but this is fundamentally different from hegemony.

### The Territorial Logic of Historical Capitalism

The progression of "containers of power" housing capitalist headquarters:

1. **City-state** (Genoa) + cosmopolitan business diaspora
2. **Proto-national state** (Dutch United Provinces) + joint-stock chartered companies
3. **Multinational state** (British Empire) + globe-encircling tributary empire
4. **Continent-sized national state** (United States) + transnational corporations, military bases, institutions of world governance

Each transition involved wars, financial reversals, and spatial fixes of increasing scale. The US attempted to bring this process to its conclusion through a world state — and failed.

### The Fusion of Capitalism and Imperialism

The UK achieved a complete fusion of capitalism and imperialism — heir to both the Genoese capitalist tradition (interstate competition for mobile capital) and the Iberian imperialist tradition (territorial conquest). India's demographic and financial resources were the "pivot" of British world-financial supremacy: Indian soldiers funded by Indian taxpayers fought Britain's imperial wars, while Indian currency devaluation and the "Home Charges" turned India into the financial engine of British global dominance.

The US cycle repeated this pattern at greater scale, but with the critical difference that the US attempted to create an actual world government rather than merely dominating a balance of power.

## PART IV: LINEAGES OF THE NEW ASIAN AGE (Chapters 10–12 + Epilogue)

### The Smithian Features of China's Development

Several features distinguish China's path from Western capitalist development:

**Accumulation without dispossession**: The redistributive land reforms (starting late 1940s) broke landlord power without separating peasants from the land. The Township and Village Enterprises (TVEs) became the primary locus of industrial development precisely because they kept rural workers connected to their agricultural base. This contrasts sharply with the Western pattern where primitive accumulation (enclosures, dispossession) was the foundation of capitalist development.

**Competition over monopoly**: China's reforms emphasized intensification of competition through breakup of monopolies rather than privatization. The result was constant over-accumulation and downward pressure on profits — a Smithian world where capitalists are driven by competition to work in the national interest, rather than a capitalist world where the state serves capital.

**The home market first**: Following Smith's "natural" path, Deng's reforms targeted domestic economy and agriculture first (Household Responsibility System, 1978–83). Foreign trade emerged from surplus rather than driving development.

**Labor-intensive, energy-saving development**: China's competitive advantage lies not in low wages per se but in the high quality of its labor force (health, education, self-management capacity) combined with techniques that substitute inexpensive educated labor for expensive machines. This is a continuation of the East Asian "Industrious Revolution" path that Sugihara identifies as distinct from the Western "Industrial Revolution" path.

### China's "Peaceful Ascent"

China's *heping jueqi* doctrine — rooted in Politburo-commissioned historical investigations of how rising powers provoke reactions — represents a deliberate choice to avoid the German/Japanese path of violent resource competition. China's strategy resembles Sun Tzu rather than Clausewitz: patient accumulation of influence rather than winner-take-all confrontation.

President Hu Jintao's "four nos": no to hegemony, no to force, no to blocs, no to the arms race. "Four yeses": confidence building, reducing difficulties, developing cooperation, avoiding confrontation.

### The Failure of US Containment

US attempts to encircle China with military alliances largely failed. The Iraq debacle undermined US credibility precisely when it needed allies for containment:

- Singapore, Indonesia, South Korea all moved toward closer ties with Beijing
- Only Japan moved decisively toward the US — isolating Japan rather than China
- China's economic locomotive role (one-third of world import volume increase, 2001–04) made containment economically irrational for Asian states
- China's influence expanded into South Asia, West Asia, Africa, and Latin America

### The East Asian Tribute Trade System Parallel

US supremacy in East Asia after WWII was realized through transforming the periphery of the former China-centered tribute trade system into a US-centered one. Key similarities: incomparably larger domestic market of the central state; political subordination in exchange for market access and "gifts." Key difference: the US system was far more militaristic and fostered functional specialization (US provides protection, Asian vassals specialize in trade/profit).

This system was inherently unstable: the Korean War created it by excluding the PRC; defeat in Vietnam forced the US to readmit China, broadening regional integration while reducing US control.

## THE EPILOGUE: THREE FUTURES

### US Strategies Toward China

1. **Kaplan's "New Cold War"** — military encirclement via PACOM. Risks nuclear confrontation, divides Asia, forces US to make concessions to allies.

2. **Kissinger's "Co-optation"** — accommodate Chinese power, integrate China into reformed US-centered order. Preserves Northern dominance but harder to sell domestically given Sinophobia.

3. **Pinkerton's "Happy Third"** — maneuver Asian states into conflict with each other while the US profits as arms supplier and financier (reprising the WWI/WWII playbook with Asian rather than European states). Most catastrophic outcome for the global South.

### The New Bandung Possibility

Against all three strategies, Arrighi sees the possibility of a **"new Bandung"** — a Southern alliance with economic rather than merely ideological foundations, capable of using the global market as an instrument of South-North equalization.

The monetarist counterrevolution backfired: by forcing developing countries to accumulate massive foreign-exchange reserves as insurance against financial crises, it created conditions where Southern surpluses could be redirected from financing US deficits toward Southern development. Venezuela as "lender of last resort" reducing US influence. Saudi and Gulf capital flowing to East Asia rather than US Treasuries. India's PM Singh urging Asian nations to redirect surpluses toward Asian development.

The key question: whether China and India (together over one-third of humanity) will open a developmental path capable of emancipating not just their nations but the world from the social and ecological devastations of Western capitalism.

### The Conditional Conclusion

If China's reorientation toward balanced development succeeds — reviving traditions of self-centered market development, accumulation without dispossession, mobilization of human rather than non-human resources, government through mass participation — it could contribute decisively to a "commonwealth of civilizations" respectful of cultural differences.

If it fails, China becomes a new epicenter of social and political chaos facilitating Northern attempts to re-establish crumbling global dominance — or, in Schumpeter's phrase, helping "humanity burn up in the horrors (or glories)" of escalating violence.

## KEY THEORETICAL CONCEPTS

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Hegemony** (Gramscian) | Domination + consent; leadership perceived as serving a general interest |
| **Domination without hegemony** | Coercive rule without perceived legitimacy |
| **Signal crisis** | Hegemonic crisis that is temporarily resolved |
| **Terminal crisis** | Irreversible breakdown of hegemonic order |
| **Systemic cycles of accumulation** | M-C-M' pattern: material expansion → financial expansion → transition |
| **Belle époque** | Temporary reflation of incumbent's wealth/power during financial phase |
| **Accumulation by dispossession** | Harvey's term for ongoing primitive accumulation via finance and state power |
| **Accumulation without dispossession** | China's development model preserving peasant land access |
| **Natural path** (Smith) | Agriculture → domestic trade → manufacturing → foreign trade |
| **Unnatural path** (Smith) | Foreign trade → manufacturing → retrograde development |
| **Smithian growth** | Market expansion within existing social framework |
| **Schumpeterian/capitalist development** | Creative destruction of social frameworks |
| **Industrious Revolution** | East Asian labor-intensive, energy-saving path |
| **Industrial Revolution** | Western capital-intensive, energy-consuming path |

## KEY SOURCES ARRIGHI ENGAGES

- **Adam Smith** — *The Wealth of Nations*: natural vs. unnatural developmental paths
- **Karl Marx** — *Capital*, *Grundrisse*: primitive accumulation, general formula M-C-M'
- **Joseph Schumpeter** — creative destruction, financial "headquarters" of capitalism
- **Fernand Braudel** — financial expansions as "sign of autumn," capitalism triumphing when it becomes the state
- **Antonio Gramsci** — hegemony vs. domination
- **Hannah Arendt** — endless accumulation of capital requires endless accumulation of power
- **David Harvey** — *The New Imperialism*: spatial fixes, accumulation by dispossession, capitalist vs. territorial logics
- **Robert Brenner** — *The Economics of Global Turbulence*: over-capacity, uneven development
- **Karl Polanyi** — habitation vs. improvement, double movement
- **Kaoru Sugihara** — Industrious Revolution, East Asian developmental path
- **Kenneth Pomeranz** — *The Great Divergence*
- **Ranajit Guha** — "dominance without hegemony"
- **David Calleo** — declining powers cementing slipping hegemony into exploitative domination
- **Paul Kennedy** — imperial overstretch
