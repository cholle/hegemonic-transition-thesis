# Carlota Perez — *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital: The Dynamics of Bubbles and Golden Ages* (2002)

## Core Thesis

Perez argues that capitalism evolves through **recurring ~50-year cycles** ("great surges of development"), each driven by a **technological revolution**. The full fruits of each revolution are only reaped with a significant time-lag — two to three decades of turbulent adaptation elapse between the initial irruption of new technologies and the eventual "golden age" based on them. This is not accidental but **structural to the nature of capitalism**, driven by the functional separation between **financial capital** and **production capital** and the mismatch between new techno-economic possibilities and old socio-institutional frameworks.

---

## The Five Technological Revolutions

Perez identifies five successive revolutions since the late 18th century:

1. **The Industrial Revolution** (1771 big-bang) — Britain. Cotton, iron, water power, canals.
2. **Age of Steam and Railways** (1829) — Britain, spreading to continent and USA. Steam engines, railways, iron/steel.
3. **Age of Steel, Electricity and Heavy Engineering** (1875) — USA and Germany overtaking Britain. Steel, electrical engineering, heavy chemistry.
4. **Age of Oil, Automobiles and Mass Production** (1908) — USA, spreading to Europe. Oil, automobiles, petrochemicals, mass production/Fordism.
5. **Age of Information and Telecommunications** (1971) — USA, spreading to Europe and Asia. Microprocessors, software, internet, telecom.

Each revolution is not just a cluster of new industries but brings a **new techno-economic paradigm** — a set of organizational principles, management practices, and "common sense" about how to run businesses and economies. These paradigms reshape everything from firm structure to international trade patterns.

---

## The Four-Phase Model

Each surge divides into two main periods — **Installation** and **Deployment** — separated by a critical **Turning Point**. Each period contains two phases:

### Installation Period (Financial capital leads)

**1. Irruption** — The new technologies burst onto a scene still dominated by the old paradigm. Venture capital floods in. New entrepreneurs emerge. The economy splits between dynamic new sectors and stagnating old ones. There's an "opportunity explosion" and a "love affair" between financial capital and the technological revolution.

**2. Frenzy** — Financial capital becomes convinced it can thrive on its own, decoupling from production. A casino economy develops. Asset inflation spirals. Infrastructure gets massively overbuilt (but this will prove useful later). Corruption flourishes. Income inequality widens dramatically. Paper values divorce from real economic performance. The bubble inflates until structural tensions become unsustainable.

### The Turning Point

A crash (or series of crashes) deflates the bubble. This is a crossroads — a space for social rethinking. Financial capital, humbled by collapse, becomes willing to accept regulation it had previously rejected. The specific institutional response determines whether what follows is a golden age, a gilded age, or a prolonged depression. The pendulum swings from extreme individualism toward collective well-being and regulatory intervention.

### Deployment Period (Production capital leads)

**3. Synergy** — With appropriate regulation in place, financial and production capital recouple. Growth becomes widespread rather than concentrated in a few sectors. The paradigm generalizes across the economy. Income distribution improves. Full employment becomes possible. This is the "golden age" — capitalism's best face, when individual and collective interests roughly coincide. Innovation shifts from exploration to consolidation, market expansion, and cost reduction.

**4. Maturity** — Diminishing returns set in. Core industries saturate their markets. Profits still flow but investment opportunities narrow. "Idle money" accumulates, searching for outlets. Financial capital begins lending to distant places and funding radical new technologies — planting the seeds of the next revolution and the next cycle of turbulence.

---

## Financial Capital vs. Production Capital

This is the book's most original contribution. Perez draws a sharp functional distinction:

**Production capital** is wealth directly engaged in generating goods and services — it operates with long time horizons, organizational commitment, and knowledge of specific industries.

**Financial capital** is wealth in liquid form — mobile, short-term oriented, seeking maximum returns wherever they can be found. It includes banks, venture capital, stock markets, and all forms of investable wealth.

These two forms of capital have a **recurring dance** across each surge:

- In **Irruption**, financial capital provides essential risk funding for revolutionary new ventures
- In **Frenzy**, it decouples from production and creates a speculative casino
- At the **Turning Point**, crashes force a reckoning and shift of power
- In **Synergy**, financial capital returns to its "intermediary" role supporting production
- In **Maturity**, idle money once again becomes restless, funding the seeds of the next revolution

The tension between these two forms of capital is what drives the recurring pattern of bubble, crash, regulation, and golden age.

---

## The Structural Tensions

Three key tensions build up during the Installation period, especially during Frenzy, that must be resolved at the Turning Point:

1. **Paper values vs. real wealth** — Stock market valuations detach from actual productive capacity. Only strict regulation can bring profit expectations back to realistic levels.

2. **Production capacity vs. demand** — Income concentration during Frenzy creates premature market saturation. The new industries have enormous potential but insufficient consumers. Policies affecting income distribution and government demand become crucial.

3. **New economy vs. old economy** — The split between dynamic high-tech sectors and stagnating traditional industries creates social polarization, unemployment, and political instability.

## The Epilogue: Writing at the Turning Point (2002)

Perez wrote the book as the NASDAQ bubble had just collapsed. She diagnosed the world as being at the Turning Point of the fifth surge — the Information Age. The Installation period (1971–2001) was over. The question was whether appropriate institutional restructuring would produce a golden age of widespread prosperity based on information technology, or whether inadequate reform would yield only a "gilded age" or prolonged stagnation.

She called for institutional imagination, particularly in financial regulation (citing the Enron/WorldCom scandals as symptoms of Frenzy's structural pressures), demand-side policies to activate markets for IT-related goods and services, and global institutional frameworks adequate to a globalized paradigm. She noted that unlike previous surges, the fifth involved globalization from the outset, making the international dimension of institutional reform uniquely critical.

## Key Analytical Connections to Other Frameworks

### Perez ↔ Hudson (Dollar Hegemony / Finance as Parasite)

The relationship between financial and production capital maps onto Hudson's analysis of finance parasitizing the productive economy — but Perez frames it as a **recurring structural feature** of capitalism rather than a terminal condition. Hudson's "protection racket" dollar system is essentially what happens when financial capital dominates without a proper Turning Point institutional recomposition.

### Perez ↔ Arrighi (Systemic Cycles of Accumulation)

Arrighi's "financial expansion" as a signal of hegemonic decline is essentially the **Frenzy/Maturity dynamic at the world-system level**. Perez focuses on the technological content; Arrighi on the geopolitical container. The two are complementary: Arrighi explains *why* the hegemon financializes (declining productive advantage), Perez explains *how* it happens and what the technological substrate looks like.

### Perez ↔ Wallerstein (Terminal Crisis of Capitalism)

Wallerstein's terminal crisis thesis could be read as the argument that the Perez cycle itself is breaking down — that capitalism can no longer generate the Turning Point institutional reforms needed to move from Installation to Deployment, because the political conditions for such reforms (a functioning state willing to regulate capital) have been systematically dismantled.

### The Post-2000 Anomaly: Why No Golden Age?

The model explains why the post-2000 period never produced a proper "golden age" — the institutional recomposition at the Turning Point was inadequate. Instead of proper regulation and demand expansion, the response was more financialization (housing bubble, QE), producing what Perez would call a **"gilded age" at best** — or arguably an extended, dysfunctional Turning Point that never resolved.

### Current Application (2025–2026)

The current situation — with US military interventions to secure energy resources, the dollar system under pressure, and China leading in solar/EV/battery technology — maps onto the **overlap between the maturity/exhaustion of the 5th surge's Installation-without-proper-Deployment** and potentially the **gestation of a 6th revolution** (energy transition? AI?), with the critical complication that the institutional recomposition Perez called for in 2002 never properly happened. The system is attempting to deploy a paradigm without having built the institutional framework to support it — while simultaneously facing the gestation of the next one.
